# Particle analyst — Type-P grains in Fluid-W

Each snippet below is an independent observation record. Cite by the
bracketed ID when producing findings.

## [particle_01]

A Type-P grain suspended in Fluid-W, observed through a microscope over a
fixed interval, traces out an erratic zig-zag path. Lengthening the
observation window produces a larger net displacement on average: grains
observed for longer intervals end up farther from their initial position
than grains observed for shorter intervals. In the absence of external
forcing, no preferred direction is apparent: averaged across many grains,
the net displacement vector is statistically indistinguishable from zero,
however long the observation window.

## [particle_02]

If the recorded path of a single Type-P grain is split into consecutive
equal-length segments, the displacement of each segment shows no detectable
statistical dependence on the previous segment's displacement. Correlation
coefficients between consecutive segment displacements are not
distinguishable from zero at the confidence levels typical of these
experiments. The grain's next observed position is well-predicted by its
current position alone; earlier history, conditional on the current
position, contributes no detectable predictive information.

## [particle_03]

Histograms of displacement, compiled across many short measurement windows
of equal duration, are symmetric about zero and single-peaked. Observed
displacements along orthogonal axes appear statistically uncorrelated, and
each axis individually reproduces the same symmetric single-peaked profile.
The spread of the histogram grows with the length of the measurement
window, but the shape of the distribution — symmetric, single-peaked,
centered at zero — is preserved across window lengths.

## [particle_04]

The observed motion has no detectable memory beyond the current position:
whether segments are sampled at 1 ms, 10 ms, or 100 ms intervals, the
qualitative statistical structure is preserved. Finer sampling reveals the
same structure at smaller scale, consistent with a continuous-time limit of
increasingly fine discrete observations. Rescaling the time axis and the
spatial axis together, by appropriately matched factors, leaves the
statistical structure of the trajectory ensemble unchanged.
